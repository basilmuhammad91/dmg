We wanted to take action and change things. https://www.vinci-construction.com/en/news/waste-management-s2r/ Thu, 18 Mar 2021 08:00:00 +0000 https://www.vinci-construction.com/?p=4022 How did S2R come to rethink the way it manages waste from its operations?

Faouzi Ben-Abdallah: For many years, I had been giving some thought to how we could redesign our waste management process. We came to an obvious conclusion when examining our building sites: demolition and refurbishment work generates enough rubble to fill a good number of skips. Sanitary appliances, tiles, windows, doors, paint, glue… a good third of what we put into the dumpster is still in good condition. Unfortunately, everything would go to the waste facility and end up in a landfill. We were really not happy with this. We did not want to sit idly by and decided to do something to change the situation. I got in touch with several people, including members of Synergie Péi, a non-profit organisation on Reunion Island which is working with regional authorities and businesses as part of an environmental sustainability initiative that promotes the recycling and reclaiming of residual materials. We got our heads together to work on viable solutions. Working with other businesses, we came up with the idea of a “building materials library”, i.e. a place where we could store the materials that still have life in them and sell them at bargain prices. So far though, we have not found a suitable place, nor the funds to cover the rental fee. Since we really wanted this project to take off, we came up with the idea of starting a yard sale.

So how did the yard sale work?

Faouzi Ben-Abdallah: We decided on a temporary event, which settled the question of premises. It was a great success. We organised the first yard sale in November 2019 at the port, during the European Week for Waste Reduction. Everything was bought up in the space of three days! On our side, the proceeds from the sale were enough to cover all the costs of organising the event. We offered both used and unused materials from our worksites, which otherwise would have been destroyed and/or sent to a landfill. By selling them to private individuals and companies at bargain prices, we put them back in the loop. It is a real virtuous cycle as regards the materials, and a real service to buyers who can make significant savings. In 2020, we even accelerated the pace and started organising these yard sales on a monthly basis. To give you an idea of the volumes involved, approximately 7 tonnes of residual materials were sold at the 4 yard sales that took place between August and November, corresponding to more than 150 different assorted items, such as toilets, bathtubs, washbasins, lighting fixtures, doors, sinks, taps, kitchen countertops… Because of the current health protocols, we organised a time-slot reservation system in order to avoid crowds forming. And it worked very well.

What are the next steps?

Faouzi Ben-Abdallah: At the end of December 2020, we responded to the Call for Expressions of Interest issued by the Innovation Plan for the French Overseas Territories, which is part of the Investment Plan led by the General Secretariat for Investment. We set up this project with a number of other partners, including the University of Reunion Island, several research laboratories and the island’s main subsidised housing company. Entitled REHABILIS, the project aims to provide a sustainable solution to the problem of construction waste (fittings, furniture, materials, etc.) by turning this waste into a source of social and economic wealth. Our proposal focuses on three areas. Firstly, the circular economy: our goal is to organise the sector to make it easier to recover and recondition residual materials before putting them back into the loop by reselling them to private individuals or businesses. That is the idea behind the “materials library” for which we want to find a permanent location. This should lead to new jobs, as we will likely hire new employees as part of our social integration approach. The second area consists in converting waste into raw materials that can be reused on our worksites. An example is the residual glass wool from our worksites, which can be turned into a bio-composite thermal insulation material when combined with bio-sourced coconut fibres. Finally, the third area of focus is pilot projects. The idea is to identify construction projects on the island, to get in touch with the design team, and share with them the list of all available residual materials on our projects, such as sanitary appliances or windows. They would then be able to incorporate these low-cost materials into their designs. The “waste” that we produce would therefore be given a life extension. For the time being, we are waiting to find out if our project will be selected. But we are optimistic, because it offers relevant strategies that can have a true impact on the economy, on employment and on the environment, in a very short time.

• 26 April 1986 / Reactor number 4 at Chernobyl nuclear plant explodes.  

• 1992 / Ukraine launches a design contest for a shelter to confine the nuclear reactor and minimise all potential risk. Resolution, a European consortium led by Campenon Bernard SGE, wins the contest. The works are entrusted to Novarka, a consortium encompassing VINCI Construction Grands Projets (leader) and Bouygues Travaux Publics.

• 2010 / Works begin, led by VINCI Construction Grands Projets. The confinement arch is designed to protect dismantling work on the former sarcophagus and reactor ruins, while maximising protection for workers on site.

• 2016 / The arch is built in an area next to the reactor to protect teams from radiations. In November 2016, the are was slide on 327 metres, thanks to a special sliding system composed of 224 hydraulic cylinders, in order to cover the damaged reactor. It took 40 hours, spread over 5 days, to complete this operation. It is the largest land mobile structure built to date.

• 2017 / Work is completed and the arch is handed over to confine, sort and stock short-lived waste, and stockpile radioactive waste.

• 2019 / Delivery of the project. This is the end of 12 years of extraordinary construction work under atypical conditions. More than 10,000 NOVARKA workers and technicians took turns working 33 million hours to complete the construction of the containment building for the damaged reactor No. 4.

A mock-up to confirm the bed bay layout

Construction companies, local authorities, the Army, the NHS and the entire supply chain worked hand in hand 24/7 for two weeks to meet the deadlines. According to John Fowler, Project Manager at VINCI Construction UK, “One of the key messages was: ‘Forget all you know about normal healthcare construction – this is about constant problem-solving.’” When his teams were ready to lay the flooring, the layout and exact dimensions of each bed bay had not yet been decided on. A full-scale mock-up bed bay was assembled to test out and confirm the dimensions based on healthcare professionals’ recommendations. To take medical constraints into account, the layout of the beds was marked out on the floor with tape, enabling everybody to visualise the points of detail. For instance, the three zones in the hospital had to be able to operate with staff and patients to move around without the Amber zone (a breakout space for staff) or the Green zone (for supplies and deliveries) entering into contact with the Red zone (the area with Covid patients). Less than two weeks after the go-ahead, a temporary facility, the Nightingale North West hospital, was ready for use on 13 April. Equipped to receive coronavirus patients requiring treatment but not intensive care, the hospital immediately eased pressure on intensive care units in existing hospitals across the region. 

Alan Kondys, Healthcare sector director at VINCI Construction UK and Framework director at Integrated Health Projects explains: “The seriousness of the COVID-19 pandemic is putting pressure on the NHS, its clinicians, and key staff. IHP had no hesitation in responding to the call to deliver the Manchester surge facility, and we are working collaboratively under ProCure22*, which is supporting this initiative nationwide.”

* ProCure22 is the construction procurement framework administrated by the NHS England & NHSE Improvements.

Integrated Health Projects (IHP) is a joint venture between VINCI Construction UK and Sir Robert McAlpine. Providing development, design, construction and facility management services for healthcare facilities, IHP has successfully completed over 170 projects since it was established in 2003.

]]> Flashback to 1998: the Vasco de Gama bridge in Lisbon is inaugurated. https://www.vinci-construction.com/en/news/flashback-to-1998-the-vasco-de-gama-bridge-in-lisbon-is-inaugurated/ Wed, 10 Feb 2021 07:51:00 +0000 https://www.vinci-construction.com/?p=3750 Designed and built under the leadership of Campenon Bernard SGE, a number of challenges stood in the way of the project. Since it opened, it has been durably contributing to the development of Lisbon’s southern bank, while alleviating local traffic congestion. Let’s look back at the project.

The clock was ticking

When the Portuguese government issued its international call for tender, it made it clear that the bridge was to be inaugurated in time for the March 1998 Lisbon Universal Exhibition. With work starting up in January 1995, the deadline proved very tight, particularly in view of the project’s technical complexity. And yet the move paid off and the Vasco de Gama Bridge was delivered on time. A source of national pride, the engineering structure also won the award for civil engineering at the Hispano-American Architecture and Civil Engineering Biennial in Madrid in 2000.

Multiple challenges

In addition to an extremely tight construction schedule, the Vasco de Gama bridge combined a number of other challenges. Due to its unprecedented length of 17 km, the curvature of the earth had to be taken into account and appropriate technical and technological solutions had to be devised. In addition, the structure was designed to withstand earthquakes 4.5 times stronger than the one that razed Lisbon to the ground in 1755. Finally, the design of the bridge, with cable-stays reaching up to 824 metres, allows it to withstand wind speeds of up to 250 km/h.

Resilience

To span the 17 km of the Tagus, the bridge was segmented into five different parts: four viaducts and a cable-stayed bridge, in addition to the interchanges. Given the tight deadlines and the length of the structure, a unique staging programme and satellite coordination system was set up. Different solutions work in combination to protect the structure from seismic risks: the northern viaduct is equipped with a shock absorber system capable of absorbing the effects of displacement. As for the deck of the cable-stayed bridge, it is not fixed to its pylons, which allows it to move laterally and longitudinally. Its flexible piers were designed for long periods of vibration. As for the piers of the central viaduct, they are equipped with hydraulic couplings which allow the bridge to move when in use but block it in the event of an earthquake.

An innovative approach

The bridge is also noteworthy in terms of its impact on the environment. Novel measures were taken at the time which contributed to promoting a new approach to assessing the environmental impact of the structures. Noise barriers, scour protection systems to protect the canal pier base and stormwater treatment basins were built in an effort to optimise and limit the impact of the bridge. On the southern bank, particular care was taken to conserve the Samouco salt marshes. These wetlands are a nature reserve and are vital to hundreds of species of birds, fish, crustaceans and molluscs. As part of the project, a new 400 ha reserve was created. In the spring of 1995, work was suspended for three weeks to allow for the passage of migratory birds.

Q1/How is working underwater different?

In most of the work we do, good manual dexterity is all that is required. But a good diver is above all someone who can see underwater, because contrary to what you might think, visibility is often close to zero. It is called working in “black water”. It is important be able to control yourself in all circumstances and to follow rules, diving safety standards and instructions to the letter.     Extruded through the print head of a 3D printing robot, the concrete is deposited progressively in a series of successive layers, entirely removing any need for formwork or moulds. The printed layer must consolidate quickly enough to prevent it collapsing during the printing process, and yet remain fresh enough for each successive layer to adhere to the previous one.

    “This layered printing process gives engineers the opportunity to use form to benefit performance, and gives architects enormous three-dimensional design freedom.”, says Romaric Quentin, R&D and Innovation Engineer at VINCI Construction.

    Using 3D concrete printing to build in a more resource-efficient world

    In the three years from 2011 to 2013, China consumed as much cement as the USA did during the entire 20th century, according to figures from the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation (Gatesnotes, USGS). And worldwide, demand is soaring exponentially: OECD forecasts suggest that Africa is likely to build 90 billion square metres of new floor space by 2060, and China 230 billion. The use of concrete is also resulting in a worrying shortage of sand.

    Minimising the use of construction materials, and therefore raw materials, building without the use of lost formwork, avoiding the need for transport by printing components as close as possible to construction sites (or even on site), cutting worksite noise pollution and reducing health and safety risks: 3D concrete printing could achieve all these aims, at the same time as contributing to the research and development effort to develop more sustainable technologies.

    The modular forms that can be created using digital technology and the additive printing process provide encouragement to reconsider the very design of our buildings. A new-generation wall could incorporate insulation into a honeycomb structure to minimise the use of both cement and insulation. Similarly, it opens up new possibilities in the design phase, such as the use of topological optimisation algorithms to improve the mechanical performance of tomorrow’s structures.

    Nevertheless, the technique is not suitable for every application: “For the time being, this remains a niche market”, explains Chloé Clair, Innovation and Engineering Director at VINCI Construction. “Identifying applications for 3D concrete printing requires explicit and complex use cases”.

    An innovation yet to be standardised and certified

    Although 3D concrete printing is a promising technique in many ways, its practical application is hampered by mechanical characteristics that have yet to be precisely determined. VINCI Construction is working with CSTB (the French Scientific and Technical Centre for Building) and CERIB (the French Centre for Concrete Industry Studies & Research) to assess its performance on the basis of ongoing experiments, and to work towards standard compliance of the process and the construction components it produces.
